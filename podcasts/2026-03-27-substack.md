## 1. Anthropic's Next Model "Mythos" Just Leaked — And They're Calling It a "Step Change"

[Fortune exclusive](https://fortune.com/2026/03/26/anthropic-says-testing-mythos-powerful-new-ai-model-after-data-leak-reveals-its-existence-step-change-in-capabilities/)

Anthropic didn't announce this one. It leaked. A data exposure revealed the existence of a model in testing called Mythos, and when Fortune asked, Anthropic confirmed it — using deliberate language: *"step change in capabilities."*

That's not casual phrasing. In the AI world, step change means something meaningfully different is possible on the other side of it. Not a speed improvement. Not a longer context window. A qualitative shift.

If you're making architecture decisions about which model to build on right now, don't lock anything in yet. The next few weeks may give you significantly more to work with.

## 2. Anthropic Says It Will Never Run Ads on Claude. Here's Why That's a Bigger Deal Than It Sounds.

[Anthropic News](https://www.anthropic.com/news)

Anthropic published an explanation this week for why they will never run advertising on Claude. The argument isn't "ads are bad" — it's structural. When an AI's revenue depends on surfacing certain content, the model is no longer fully working for you. It's working for whoever paid for the placement. Once that incentive exists, you can't fully trust the outputs.

This is a direct challenge to the rest of the industry. Most consumer AI products haven't ruled out advertising. Several are backed by companies whose primary business model has historically been ads.

Three practical implications:

If you build on Claude, you now have an explicit architectural guarantee that outputs aren't being shaped by advertiser interests.

If you're in marketing or sales and thinking about AI as a reach channel — the Anthropic surface area is not going to be one. Plan accordingly.

And if you're designing the business model for your own AI product, Anthropic just published the strongest version of the anti-ads argument. Worth reading before you finalize anything.

## 3. Someone Built an Agentic Job Search Workflow — And If You Work in Recruiting, You Need to Know About It

[I built an agentic workflow for job search](https://notakevin.substack.com/p/i-built-an-agentic-workflow-for-job)

Someone published a full agentic job search workflow on Substack three days ago, and it hasn't gotten nearly enough attention.

It's not a chatbot. Not a resume template. It's a multi-step agent that handles the most time-consuming parts of a job search automatically: analyzing job postings for fit, rewriting resume bullets to match the role, and drafting personalized LinkedIn outreach. The full technical breakdown is in the post.

If you're in recruiting or talent acquisition, the angle that matters isn't how to build it — it's what it means for your process.

When candidates use tools like this, your signal-to-noise problem gets harder. Resumes look more polished. Cover letters feel more thoughtful. LinkedIn messages seem more relevant. And behind many of them is an agent that did in 10 minutes what used to take three hours.

Two adaptations worth making now: First, recalibrate what a well-written application actually signals — it's no longer a reliable proxy for motivation or fit. Second, lean harder into parts of your process that agents can't easily replicate: live conversations, work samples, situational questions that require genuine knowledge of the role.

This isn't a future problem. It's a now problem.
