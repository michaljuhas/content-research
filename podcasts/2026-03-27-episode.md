## SEGMENT 1 — Anthropic's Next Model "Mythos" Just Leaked — And They're Calling It a "Step Change"
*Source: https://fortune.com/2026/03/26/anthropic-says-testing-mythos-powerful-new-ai-model-after-data-leak-reveals-its-existence-step-change-in-capabilities/*

Anthropic didn't announce this one. It leaked.

A data exposure this week revealed that Anthropic has a new model in testing called "Mythos." And when Fortune reached out, Anthropic confirmed it — and used very deliberate language. They called it a "step change in capabilities."

That phrase matters. In the AI world, "step change" is not casual. It's not "we made it slightly faster" or "we improved the context window." Step change means something meaningfully different is possible on the other side of it. Better reasoning, longer context, stronger agentic behavior — we don't know the specifics yet, but that framing is intentional.

Now, Claude's current lineup is already competitive with GPT and Gemini on most benchmarks. If Mythos represents a genuine step up from that, it could shift what's realistic to build — especially for agent workflows that currently hit walls around multi-step reasoning.

Here's the practical angle: if you're in the middle of making architecture decisions around which model to build on — don't lock anything in yet. The next two to four weeks may give you significantly more to work with. And if you're already on Claude, this is a reason to stay close to Anthropic's release cadence right now.

## SEGMENT 2 — Anthropic Says It Will Never Run Ads on Claude. Here's Why That's a Bigger Deal Than It Sounds.
*Source: https://www.anthropic.com/news*

Anthropic published something this week that I think is worth sitting with — an explanation of why they will never run advertising on Claude.

The argument isn't just "ads are bad." It's structural. The core claim is that advertising creates a fundamental conflict of interest between the model and the user. When an AI's revenue depends on showing you certain content, the AI is no longer fully working for you. It's working for whoever paid for that placement. And once that incentive exists, it's very hard to fully trust the model's outputs.

This is a direct shot at the rest of the industry. Most AI products — especially consumer-facing ones — haven't ruled out advertising. And several of them are backed by companies that have historically relied on ads as a primary revenue model. Anthropic is staking out a clear position: that is incompatible with a genuinely helpful assistant.

What does it mean practically? A few things.

First, if you're building on Claude, you're building on a platform that has explicitly committed to not optimizing outputs for advertiser interests. That's a real and meaningful architectural guarantee.

Second, for anyone in marketing or sales who's thinking about how to reach people through AI — this signals that the Anthropic surface area is not going to be an ad channel. Budget accordingly.

And third, if you're thinking about your own AI product and how to monetize it — Anthropic just published the strongest version of the anti-ads argument. Worth reading before you finalize your business model.

## SEGMENT 3 — Someone Built an Agentic Job Search Workflow — And If You Work in Recruiting, You Need to Know About It
*Source: https://notakevin.substack.com/p/i-built-an-agentic-workflow-for-job*

This one is three days old and it hasn't gotten nearly enough attention.

Someone built a full agentic workflow for job searching. Not a chatbot. Not a resume template. An actual multi-step agent that handles the most time-consuming parts of a job search automatically: analyzing job postings for alignment with your background, rewriting resume bullets to match the role, and drafting personalized LinkedIn outreach messages.

The full technical breakdown is on Substack. It's a real build, it's working, and it represents something I think will become very common in the next 12 months.

Here's the thing: every recruiter and hiring manager on this show should pay attention to this — not because you're going to build it yourself, but because candidates are starting to use things like this against your screening process.

When a hundred candidates apply for a role and forty of them have AI-tailored resumes and AI-personalized outreach, your signal-to-noise problem gets a lot worse. The resumes look better. The cover letters sound more thoughtful. The LinkedIn messages feel more relevant. And behind many of them is an agent that took 10 minutes to do what used to take three hours.

The adaptation on your side is two things. First, recalibrate what "a well-written application" actually signals — it's no longer a reliable proxy for motivation or fit. Second, lean harder into parts of the process that agents can't easily replicate: live conversations, work samples, specific situational questions that require genuine knowledge of the role.

This shift is happening now. It's not hypothetical.
