## 1. Claude Can Now Control Your Mac

[VentureBeat](https://venturebeat.com/technology/anthropics-claude-can-now-control-your-mac-escalating-the-fight-to-build-ai)

Anthropic just gave Claude the ability to operate your Mac — clicking buttons, filling forms, opening apps, managing files — without you touching the keyboard. OpenAI has been working on something similar with Operator. Now Anthropic is in the same race.

The question isn't whether this is possible anymore. It is. Start with the task you do most often that requires no judgment — just steps. That's your first autonomous desktop workflow.

## 2. Mistral Forge: Your Company's Knowledge Becomes the Model

[Mistral announcement](https://mistral.ai/news/forge)

Mistral launched Forge — a product that lets companies train a frontier-grade model on their own proprietary data. Not a chatbot wrapper. Not RAG. The company's internal knowledge, transcripts, and documents *become* the model itself.

This is aimed at enterprises that won't send data to OpenAI or Anthropic — healthcare, legal, finance, government. For people building AI products for clients, it opens a new pricing tier: instead of a generic AI layer, you're offering something that has genuinely absorbed your client's institutional knowledge.

## 3. Sam Altman Just Disclosed a 50-Gigawatt Energy Deal

[Sam Altman on X](https://x.com/sama/status/2036137695605563682)

Sam Altman stepped off the board of Helion Energy — a fusion startup he personally backed — because OpenAI is exploring buying power from them. Potentially 50 gigawatts by 2035. That's roughly the entire electricity consumption of the United Kingdom.

This disclosure accidentally reveals something enormous: OpenAI is planning compute infrastructure at a scale that competes with national power grids. The models you'll build on in 2028–2030 will make today's look like early smartphones. Whatever you're building now, design it so it can take advantage of dramatically more capable AI — not just today's version.

## 4. A Viral App, 4,000 Users, and a Crash: What Dan Shipper Actually Built

[Dan Shipper on X](https://x.com/danshipper/status/2035030133413704154)

Dan Shipper vibe-coded an AI doc editor called Proof. It went viral. 4,000+ documents created in under two days. Then it went down.

Two lessons. First: building something that works and building something that holds up are two different problems. Rate limiting, queuing, and error handling need to exist before you go viral — not after.

Second: Shipper used Codex agents to debug the live outage. AI coding agents aren't just for greenfield features. They're for production firefighting. If you haven't used Claude Code or Codex on a real bug under pressure, that's worth practising.

## 5. Naval Ravikant: AI Apps Are "The Beginning of the End for iPhone's Dominance"

[Naval on X](https://x.com/naval/status/2036285641462595898)

Naval posted two ideas this week worth sitting with.

First: AI coding agents can deliver a fully custom app to your phone with a single prompt — no App Store required. He calls it "the beginning of the end for iPhone's dominance."

Second: *"A lot of software is about to get a lot better, right before it becomes unnecessary."*

That second one is sharper. If software can be generated on demand for your exact situation, the App Store becomes a directory for legacy products. The moat moves from distribution to knowledge, taste, and — especially — data. Domain experts who can direct AI precisely are more valuable than ever. The people who get disrupted are those building generic tools for generic problems.

## 6. LiteLLM Was Hit by a Supply Chain Attack. Check Your Dependencies.

[Simon Willison on X](https://x.com/simonw/status/2036451896970584167)

Simon Willison flagged this today: LiteLLM — one of the most widely used Python libraries for routing AI API calls — was compromised via a malicious PyPI update.

If you use LiteLLM, check your dependency version now. Three simple things to do regardless: pin your AI library versions in `requirements.txt`, set up alerts for new releases on packages you depend on, and don't auto-update AI libraries in production without reviewing the diff. As AI tooling becomes infrastructure, it becomes a target. Treat it accordingly.
