## SEGMENT 1 — Claude Can Now Control Your Mac
*Source: https://venturebeat.com/technology/anthropics-claude-can-now-control-your-mac-escalating-the-fight-to-build-ai*

Anthropic just gave Claude the ability to take over your Mac. Not just answer questions — actually click buttons, fill forms, open apps, manage files. All without you touching the keyboard.

This is called computer use, and it means Claude can now run workflows end-to-end on your desktop. OpenAI has been working on a similar thing called Operator. Now Anthropic is in the same race.

The practical angle: think about what you do manually on your Mac every day. Copying data between tabs, sending follow-up emails, updating a spreadsheet after a call, filing documents. That's the stuff Claude can now do for you autonomously.

The question isn't whether this is possible anymore. It is. The question is: what do you delegate first? Start with the task you do most often that requires no judgment — just steps. That's your first Claude Mac workflow.

## SEGMENT 2 — Mistral Launches Forge: Your Company's Knowledge Becomes the Model
*Source: https://mistral.ai/news/forge*

Mistral just launched a product called Forge. The idea: a company takes its own proprietary data — internal docs, transcripts, product knowledge, customer history — and Mistral trains a frontier-grade model on it. Not a chatbot wrapper. Not RAG. The data *becomes* the model.

This is aimed squarely at enterprises that can't or won't send their data to OpenAI or Anthropic. And there are many of them — healthcare, legal, finance, government.

For people building AI products for clients, this opens a new tier. Instead of prompting a generic model that gives generic answers, you could offer clients a model that has genuinely absorbed their institutional knowledge. That's a fundamentally different value proposition — and a different pricing conversation.

The question worth asking your clients right now: what knowledge does your company have that a generic AI model doesn't? That's what Forge is designed to capture.

## SEGMENT 3 — Sam Altman Just Disclosed a 50-Gigawatt Energy Deal
*Source: https://x.com/sama/status/2036137695605563682*

Sam Altman posted this week that he's stepping off the board of Helion Energy — a fusion startup he personally invested in — because OpenAI is now exploring buying power from them. Potentially 50 gigawatts by 2035.

Fifty gigawatts. That's roughly the entire electricity consumption of the United Kingdom.

This is a conflict-of-interest disclosure that accidentally reveals something enormous: OpenAI is planning compute infrastructure at a scale that competes with national power grids. They're not waiting for the energy market to catch up. They're going directly to a fusion startup.

For builders, the signal here is simple: the labs are betting that AI compute will be orders of magnitude larger in 3–5 years than it is today. The models you'll be building on in 2028 or 2030 will make today's models look like early smartphones. Whatever you're building now, design it so it can take advantage of dramatically more capable AI — not just today's version.

## SEGMENT 4 — A Viral App, 4,000 Users, and a Crash: What Dan Shipper Actually Built
*Source: https://x.com/danshipper/status/2035030133413704154*

Dan Shipper built a vibe-coded AI doc editor called Proof. It went viral. 4,000+ documents created in under two days. Then it went down.

He documented the whole arc in real time on X — the excitement, the infrastructure wall, and then using Codex agents to debug the crash in production.

There are two lessons here. First: building something that works and building something that holds up are two completely different problems. Most vibe-coded apps hit the first milestone and forget about the second. The ops layer — rate limiting, queuing, error handling — needs to exist before you go viral, not after.

Second lesson: Shipper used Codex subagents to debug the live outage. That's the part that doesn't get talked about enough. AI coding agents aren't just for greenfield features. They're for production firefighting. If you haven't tried using Claude Code or Codex on a real bug in a real system under pressure, that's the next thing to practice.

## SEGMENT 5 — Naval Ravikant: AI Apps Are "The Beginning of the End for iPhone's Dominance"
*Source: https://x.com/naval/status/2036285641462595898*

Naval Ravikant posted two takes this week that I keep thinking about.

First: AI coding agents can now deliver a fully custom app to your phone with a single prompt — one-shot, no App Store. That's, he says, "the beginning of the end for iPhone's dominance."

Second: *"A lot of software is about to get a lot better, right before it becomes unnecessary."*

That second one is the sharper insight. The App Store model — where you discover, download, and manage apps — is built on the assumption that software is a product. But if software can be generated on demand for your exact situation, in the moment you need it, the App Store becomes a directory for legacy products.

For people who've built careers on software — and most of you listening have — this could mean a slight repositioning. The moat moves from distribution to knowledge, taste, and ESPECIALLY data. An experienced recruiter, marketer, or ops person who understands their domain deeply and can direct AI precisely is more valuable than ever. The people who get disrupted are the ones building generic tools for generic problems.
