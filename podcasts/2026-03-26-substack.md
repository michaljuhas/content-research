# AI That Works Without You — And What Happens When Nobody's Watching

**AI with Michal · March 26, 2026**


The audio is above. Below is a written summary you can skim, share, or save for later.

Five stories today with a common thread: AI is increasingly acting on its own — researching, coding, hiring, building. The question is no longer whether it can. It's what you do when it drifts, degrades, or quietly gets worse while you're looking the other way.

## 1. An AI Just Published a Peer-Reviewed Paper in Nature. Without a Human Researcher.

[Towards end-to-end automation of AI research — Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10265-5)

An AI system built by researchers at UBC, Sakana AI, and the Vector Institute just had a paper published in *Nature* — the scientific community's gold standard. It generated its own hypothesis, designed and ran the experiments, wrote the paper, and passed peer review with no human researcher in the loop.

This isn't a preprint or a blog post. *Nature* doesn't publish demos. What makes this significant beyond the headline is the architecture: the system runs a loop of ideate, experiment, evaluate, and iterate — the same pattern at the core of every serious autonomous agent being built today. The fact that it works well enough to clear peer review tells you this architecture is mature enough to study and adapt.

For builders designing competitive analysis agents, content research pipelines, or any workflow that needs to self-evaluate and improve — the architecture is public in the paper. Go read it not for the science but for the system design. It's the most thoroughly validated autonomous agent loop that currently exists.

## 2. Jason Lemkin Runs 25+ Live AI Agents Every Day — and He Made a Mistake You Need to Know About

[Jason Lemkin on X](https://x.com/jasonlk/status/2035747161183580489)

SaaStr founder Jason Lemkin posted his actual daily schedule: 60 minutes reviewing agent outputs at dawn, vibe-coding new projects mid-morning, back to updating agents in production after lunch. Then he followed it up with a confession — they stopped doing daily check-ins on one agent, and it quietly degraded over time.

The lesson is simple but easy to skip: deploying an agent is 20% of the work. Monitoring it, catching drift, updating it when the world changes around it — that's the other 80%. Everyone talks about building agents. Almost no one talks about maintaining them.

If you're building AI agents for clients, make the review loop part of the contract before you ship — a 15-minute daily check-in on outputs, drift, and errors. Clients won't ask for it. But when an unmonitored agent quietly fails and costs them a deal, they'll come back to you. Build the review ritual in from day one. It's not overhead — it's the product.


## 3. Cursor Ships Self-Hosted Cloud Agents — The Enterprise Privacy Objection Just Got a Lot Weaker

[Run cloud agents in your own infrastructure — Cursor](https://cursor.com/blog/self-hosted-cloud-agents)

Cursor just shipped self-hosted cloud agents. Autonomous coding agents — cloning your repo, running parallel tasks, operating with a full terminal and browser — running entirely inside your own infrastructure, with no code ever touching Cursor's servers.

For the past two years, the main blocker to deploying agentic coding at enterprise scale has been data privacy. Healthcare, legal, finance, government — they all want the capability but won't send proprietary code to a third-party cloud. That objection just got substantially weaker.

For anyone consulting or selling AI automation to clients in regulated industries, this is a meaningful shift. The privacy conversation changes from "we can't do that here" to "how do we get started." If you've had deals stall over data sovereignty concerns, this is worth bringing back to the table.


## 4. Lenny Rachitsky: 67,000 Open Engineering Roles. AI Is Creating Tech Jobs, Not Killing Them.

[Lenny Rachitsky on X](https://x.com/lennysan/status/2036535460726767793)

Lenny Rachitsky published hiring data this week that cuts directly against the prevailing narrative. There are 67,000 open engineering roles globally right now — the highest in three years, up nearly 20% since January. PM openings are 75% above their 2023 low. Design openings, however, are flat. Completely unchanged since ChatGPT launched. Lenny's read: AI is letting engineers ship so fast there's less need to involve designers in the loop.

That structural divergence — engineering and PM up sharply, design stagnant — is the real story buried inside a jobs report. AI isn't compressing tech hiring. It's reshaping which roles companies prioritise.

If you're in recruiting, the data is giving you clear direction. Engineering and PM are where budgets are flowing. If you have a bench of candidates in those categories who've gone quiet, this week is the right time to reactivate. And if you recruit in design, the honest conversation with clients isn't about whether AI will change design roles — it already has.


## 5. Lovable Just Launched 2.0 — The $6.6B AI App Builder and What Its Success Is Really Telling You

[Lovable Review 2026](https://aitoolanalysis.com/lovable-review/)

Lovable — the AI app builder that takes you from idea to deployed product without writing code — shipped version 2.0 this week. It's no longer just a UI generator. It now handles full-stack applications: backend logic, database connections, and deployment. For non-technical builders, the gap between "I have an idea" and "I have a live product" just closed further. The company is now valued at $6.6 billion.

What's worth studying isn't just the product improvement. It's how Lovable reached that valuation without a traditional enterprise sales motion — almost entirely through word-of-mouth and user-generated demos. People post what they built, others see it, they sign up. That's a content strategy masquerading as organic growth.

If you're marketing a product-led AI tool, this is the playbook to study. User-generated demos of real builds — raw, specific, showing the actual output — consistently outperform polished brand marketing at this stage of the market. The highest-leverage thing you can do isn't produce better case studies. It's make your users want to show off what they built.
